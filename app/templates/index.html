Sno-King Data Project

In the spring semester of my senior year, I enrolled in the Data Science Discovery program, which connects Berkeley Data Science students with local nonprofits, researchers, and startups. Through the program, I worked with four other students on a semester-long project for Sno-King Ice Arenas, a nonprofit Ice Sports Management company that had been hit hard by COVID-19 and mandatory quarantine. The company, which owns and operates several large scale ice rinks in the greater Seattle area, had to shut down all its facilities and programs (youth hockey, figure skating, classes, etc.) for the majority of 2020, and lost out on a massive amount of its expected revenue.

As plans to reopen went underway in early 2021, we cleaned and examined all the untapped data that Sno-King had collected over the past few years, compiled a series of detailed reports, and recommended a new data management strategy for the company moving forward. I personally analyzed all donation/fundraiser data and helped indentify the demographics of SNO-King's core customers/donors, as well as those who were on the "fringe" and may be converted to donate more (and more often). Our efforts helped SNO-King create a more detailed roadmap for reopening and many of the insights we provided are still being used in the company's decision making today.

For the bulk of our cleaning and analysis, we used the Python programming language and the pandas data manipulation library. We also used seaborn to create our data visualizations, some of which were touched up in Adobe Illustrator.

My Personal Portfolio Website

After finishing undergrad, I created this portfolio website to learn more about web development and to get experience deploying a web application I built completely myself from front-end to back-end. I will continue to update it with new features (blog, styling, interactive projects, etc) and progress reports as I build my portfolio. In the process of constructing this website, I became much more familiar with front-end technologies and was able to build something (albeit simple) from scratch, customizing everything to my liking.

When I decided I wanted a Contact Page that could relay messages directly to my email, I chose to use Flask as my web framework because Python is definitely my favorite language and the one I'm most comfortable working in. The reason I opted for Flask over Django is because it's lightweight and leaves me the room and ability to make more (potentially major) changes to my website over time. However, I've never used Flask before so I had to spend a decent amount of time learning and getting used to its structure. I also used JavaScript for the dropdown menu on mobile view and for opening and closing project descriptions.

ActionMap

"ActionMap" was the penultimate project for CS169, Berkeley's intro to Software Engineering class. I worked on it during the summer 2020 session with one other student. Essentially, ActionMap is a platform that enables users to identify political representatives in their area and contribute commentary on them and the ideas they represent. Typical user flow starts with a landing page containing an interactive map of the United States. A user then clicks on their respective state, which renders a new counties map for that state. From there, clicking on a given county brings up a list of political representatives in that area that users are able to click on and read more about. Aside from viewing the basic information of these representatives (picture, party, age, email, etc.), users are also able to read, post, or comment on different web articles written about them. Users may also find representatives using the search bar, post/find information about events in their area, and create an account by signing in through either Google or GitHub.

We used Rails as the webframework for this application, following general MVC structure that was laid out for us. The bulk of the interactive map was rendered using JavaScript and TopoJSON, and we also used JQuery to listen to and react to DOM events. For each political representative's profile page, information was provided using the Google Civic Information API. As far as styling goes, we used Bootstrap for a large amount of our app's elements.
